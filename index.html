About me

Why am I doing what I'm doing?

Short answer: It's complicated; I asked myself some deeply personal and philosophical questions during the lowest point in my career (aka MBA) to distract myself from the toxicity and lack of education. In the end I concluded that the pursuit of my childhood dream (to become a scientist) will satisfy my "personal utility" goals even if it doesn't fulfill all the success criteria established by others around me.

Long answer: As short as the short answer was, it took a lot of thinking and internal debates to answer those questions in a coherent way.

Neti Neti is a concept in Hinduism that involves finding the meaning of X by negating X'. Though this is a mathematically and logically sound method, it cannot be applied in practice to find one's profession/passion because nobody has infinite time to explore all career paths and nobody would give credit to irrelevant experiences. As creates limited by the finiteness of our lifespans we are forced to make (informed or uninformed) decisive choices with small sample sizes (sometimes as low as one).

Thanks to my rigorous undergraduate education, some tough personal and professional circumstances, and some timely guidance from Prof. Joydip Mitra, I understood how I could live an enriching life (personal and professional) after wasting 2 years on MBA. I quickly realized I hadn't deviated too far away from my childhood goal, and that I could accomplish the goal if I was willing to commit blood, sweat and tears to a path that was intellectually awakening (but not so financially attractive, atleast not in the short term).

With Prof. Joydip's guidance I was able to identify Statistics as the area of interest - an area that luckily serves as a bridge in several areas of science, and almost all areas that involve data. Once I saw through the fog and saw light at the end of the tunnel, I dedicated myself to become a specialist in "everything about data". This approach may sound like a generalist or a jack-of-all-trades to some, but I consider knowing almost ALL the algorithms as a way to know multiple ways of solving the same problem - like a "Swiss army knife" approach to science.

During the pursuit of my childhood dream I created value for companies while gaining exerpience through learnings - both positive and negative.

In this era of social media everyone wants to portray themselves as the epitome of an employee. However, psychology of the human mind makes it's easier to remember negative experiences over positive experiences. I've faced a few incidents in the past where my professional skill wasn't sufficient to deliver satisfactory results. Though I don't take those issues personally, I take learnings (especially fundamentals) seriously because I strongly believe technical upskilling by improving knowledge and efficiency as the primary (but not only) fuel for professional growth.

I like keeping my non-professional interests 'orthogonal'. Outside the office I'm passionate about volleyball - I've been playing for 20+ years and made some fond memories that I occasionally share here. I watch a lot of documentaries on math and science. Purely out of personal interest I'd like to learn astrophysics, psychology, forensic science and city planning. Apart from these activities I'm likely to be on long walks through trails, biking, video gaming, watching anime, ...
